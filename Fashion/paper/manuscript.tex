% ======================= ABSTRACT =======================
\section*{Abstract}

\noindent Pseudo-diffusion (\Dstar{}) is the least identifiable parameter of the
intravoxel incoherent motion (IVIM) model, a fact long known in principle from
simulation. We show that this weak identifiability has a direct, measurable, and
reproducible fingerprint in real data, and we characterise and resolve its
consequences for uncertainty quantification using an independent, clean-room
reimplementation. On the open OSIPI TF2.4 in-vivo abdomen acquisition (Zenodo
14605039), an independently written box-constrained nonlinear least-squares (NLLS) fit
rails \Dstar{} at a fit bound in 54.2\% (95\% CI 52.0--56.4) of homogeneous-ROI
voxels, reproducing a previously reported 54.7\% without using its code. The phenomenon
generalises: it recurs in 47.8\% of the full abdominal ROI (n\,=\,19{,}652) and --- in our
primary independent cross-organ test, an unrelated organ, scanner, and site acquired with
a proper $b=0$---in a multi-subject liver DWI cohort (TCGA-LIHC) at 43.7\% (four
b-values). A sparser three-b-value liver scheme that omits $b=0$ rails still more
(73.4\%), directionally consistent with the same mechanism: thinner low-$b$ perfusion
sampling weakens \Dstar{} identifiability further, exactly as predicted. We then show how this manifests in uncertainty estimates. Replacing a
previously reported \emph{marginal} coverage figure---which we could not reproduce and
trace to an undocumented standard-deviation convention---we report \Dstar{} coverage
\emph{conditionally}, per true-\Dstar{} tercile, under an explicit honest
Cram\'er--Rao convention. Symmetric Gaussian intervals under-cover \Dstar{} specifically
in the high-\Dstar{} tercile (central-95\% coverage 0.63 Laplace, 0.81 MCMC) while
remaining near-nominal at low \Dstar{}---an identifiability-driven, conditional failure,
not a uniform one. The resolution is shape-aware: posterior 2.5/97.5 quantile intervals
restore near-nominal marginal \Dstar{} coverage (0.90), and a clean-room amortized
(neural posterior) estimator is simultaneously better-calibrated (\Dstar{} coverage 0.98
vs 0.76), lower-ECE (0.069 vs 0.121) and sharper (0.11 vs 0.18) than the railed-NLLS
baseline, all gaps with bootstrap CIs excluding zero. A residual high-\Dstar{}
under-coverage (0.81) survives even the quantile fix---the irreducible identifiability
wall. All numbers derive from a single seeded reproduction reproducible with one
command, and the open data make the primary phenomenon independently checkable. The
contribution is the first systematic real-data quantification of \Dstar{} railing across
cohorts, paired with an honest conditional-coverage characterisation and its calibration
resolution.

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} IVIM, pseudo-diffusion, parameter identifiability,
uncertainty quantification, calibration, quantile intervals, simulation-based inference,
reproducibility.

% ======================= INTRODUCTION =======================
\section*{1\quad Introduction}

Intravoxel incoherent motion (IVIM) imaging (Le Bihan et al., 1988) separates tissue
diffusion ($D$) from microvascular pseudo-diffusion (\Dstar{}) through a biexponential
signal model with perfusion fraction $f$. Of its three parameters, \Dstar{} is by far
the least identifiable: its signal contribution is weighted by $f$ (typically 5--20\%),
decays over a narrow low-$b$ window, and degrades rapidly at clinical signal-to-noise
ratio (SNR) (Koh et al., 2011; Lemke et al., 2011; Federau et al., 2012;
Gurney-Champion et al., 2018). That \Dstar{} is hard to estimate is therefore not new.
What has been missing is a demonstration that this weak identifiability leaves a
\emph{measurable, reproducible fingerprint in real acquisitions}---one that does not
depend on simulation assumptions, a particular fitting code, or ground truth---together
with an honest account of what it does to the uncertainty estimates clinicians would
act on, and how to repair it.

This paper supplies that account, and we state its novelty up front because it is the
binding contribution. \textbf{The contribution is the first systematic real-data
quantification of \Dstar{} boundary-railing across independent cohorts, paired with an
honest, conditional characterisation of the calibration failure it induces and the
shape-aware resolution of that failure.} It is explicitly \emph{not} the bare
observation that NLLS struggles on \Dstar{}, which is well established. Two one-sentence
deltas locate the work. (a) Relative to the known instability of \Dstar{}
(Gurney-Champion et al., 2018; Meeus et al., 2017), which is documented largely in
simulation and as estimator variance, we quantify instability as a concrete,
optimiser-level \emph{boundary-railing rate} that is directly readable from real data,
reproduces in an independent clean-room implementation, and recurs across scanners,
organs, and acquisition schemes with bootstrap confidence intervals. (b) Relative to
Casali et al.\ (2026), who build a comprehensive uncertainty-quantification framework
that decomposes a supervised deep network's IVIM uncertainty into aleatoric and
epistemic components, our object is different: an estimator-agnostic, real-data
identifiability \emph{phenomenon} and its conditional-coverage resolution, not a learned
network's internal uncertainty budget.

The phenomenon we measure is boundary-railing. When a bounded least-squares optimiser
cannot localise \Dstar{} from the data, it does not return a diffuse estimate---it drives
\Dstar{} to a fit bound and stops, producing a point estimate pinned at the edge of the
feasible box with a spuriously narrow apparent precision. Railing is thus a per-voxel,
assumption-free read-out of weak identifiability: it requires no ground truth and no
trust in a noise model, only the optimiser's own output. We quantify its rate on open
in-vivo data, show it generalises, and then trace its downstream effect on three
uncertainty constructions---Gaussian (Laplace and MCMC standard-deviation) credible
intervals, posterior quantile intervals, and an amortized neural posterior estimator---using
an independent reimplementation so that no number depends on the original analysis code.

A recurring hazard motivates the way we report coverage. A \emph{marginal} coverage
number, averaged over a parameter range, can understate or overstate a failure that is
concentrated in one regime, and can be made arbitrarily severe by an undocumented
convention for how a railed voxel's standard deviation is reported. We therefore report
\Dstar{} coverage conditionally---stratified by the true \Dstar{} tercile---and under an
explicitly stated Cram\'er--Rao standard-deviation convention, so that the load-bearing
claim is regime- and convention-robust rather than a single headline figure.

% ======================= METHODS =======================
\section*{2\quad Materials and methods}

All quantitative results were produced by Gnomon, a clean-room reimplementation of the
IVIM forward model, estimators, and calibration metrics written independently of any
prior project code, and were cross-checked for cross-cohort generalization by Sextant, a
separate assumption-free railing analysis. Both are seeded and reproducible with one
command (Data and code availability). The numeric source of truth for every figure in
this manuscript is Gnomon's \texttt{results/reproduction.json} and
\texttt{handoff/conditional\_coverage.json}; Sextant supplies only the cross-cohort
generalization layer.

\subsection*{2.1\quad Forward model and synthetic substrate}

Biexponential IVIM signal was modelled as
$S(b)/\Szero = (1-f)\,e^{-bD} + f\,e^{-b(D+\Dstar{})}$ (Le Bihan et al., 1988),
reimplemented from physics with an analytic Jacobian verified against finite differences.
The synthetic substrate is a nine-cell design: three physiological ground-truth triples
$(D,\Dstar{},f)$ drawn from documented priors $\times$ three SNR levels
$\{10, 20, 40\}$ $\times$ 200 Rician noise realisations, seed 20260620. Synthetic truth
is used only where ground truth is required (coverage, calibration); the primary
real-data claim uses no synthetic data.

\subsection*{2.2\quad Open in-vivo data}

The primary real-data analysis used the open OSIPI TF2.4 IVIM-MRI Code Collection
in-vivo \emph{abdomen} acquisition (Gurney-Champion et al., 2025; Zenodo record
14605039, CC-BY-4.0), fetched on demand and SHA-256-verified, never redistributed
in-tree. The acquisition is a $144\times144\times21$ volume with 104 measurements over
12 unique b-values $\{0,10,20,30,40,50,75,100,150,250,400,600\}$ s/mm$^2$ (Philips 3T).
The archive's homogeneous-tissue mask defines a 1932-voxel ROI; each voxel was
b0-normalised. A previously reported ``1618 high-SNR voxels'' corresponds to this ROI
under an unstated SNR cut; we therefore report the railing rate on the full
homogeneous ROI and on a b0-SNR$>$25 subset so the load-bearing number carries its
selection sensitivity. For cross-cohort generalization (Section 3.1), Sextant analysed
the full abdominal ROI and an independent liver DWI cohort from TCGA-LIHC (Erickson et
al., 2016; Clark et al., 2013), a different site, vendor (Siemens 1.5T), and organ, with
no IVIM ground truth, under download-on-demand with assembled arrays git-ignored.

\subsection*{2.3\quad Estimators}

\textbf{NLLS and boundary-railing.} A box-constrained four-parameter fit
(trust-region-reflective least squares, analytic Jacobian) in scaled parameters, with box
(display units) $D\in[0.2,3.0]$, $\Dstar{}\in[3,150]\times10^{-3}$ mm$^2$/s,
$f\in[0,0.5]$. A parameter is \emph{railed} iff
$|\hat{x}-\text{bound}|/(\text{upper}-\text{lower}) < \texttt{rail\_tol}$; we use
$\texttt{rail\_tol}=10^{-3}$ as primary and $10^{-2}$ as a sensitivity bound. The
\Dstar{} railing rate is the primary real-data quantity. Sextant's independent
implementation additionally reports the rail direction (lower vs upper bound) and a
generous-bound control to confirm railing is not an artefact of tight bounds.

\textbf{Bayesian posteriors.} A Laplace posterior (Gaussian at the MAP with the
Cram\'er--Rao covariance) yields a symmetric standard-deviation (SD) interval. A
clean-room random-walk Metropolis MCMC (burn 1500, keep 2000, thin 2) over a Gaussian
likelihood with the uniform-box prior yields both a symmetric SD interval and a 2.5/97.5
posterior \emph{quantile} interval (Orton et al., 2014; While, 2017).

\textbf{Amortized neural posterior.} A masked-autoregressive normalizing-flow posterior
(Papamakarios et al., 2021) was trained by simulation-based inference (Cranmer et al.,
2020) on the forward model (simulation budget 80{,}000; 40 epochs), with
simulation-based calibration checked (Talts et al., 2018). It is used here only as a
better-calibrated estimator against which to contrast the railed NLLS baseline (Section
3.3); the amortized-posterior efficiency audit against the information floor is out of
scope for this paper (Section 4).

\subsection*{2.4\quad The standard-deviation convention (load-bearing)}

How a \emph{railed} voxel's reported SD is constructed is a modelling choice that, if
left undocumented, can manufacture an apparent calibration catastrophe. We make it
explicit and report two conventions. The \emph{honest} Cram\'er--Rao convention lets the
interval widen where the Fisher information is absent---statistically the correct
behaviour for an unidentified parameter, and the one recommended here---and is the basis
for biased/regularised estimation bounds (Hero \& Fessler, 1993). The \emph{floored}
convention instead caps (floors) a railed \Dstar{}'s SD, reporting an unidentified value
as if it were precisely known---overconfident by construction. Flooring is shown only to
explain a previously reported severity; the honest convention is used for all
recommended numbers.

\subsection*{2.5\quad Calibration metrics and uncertainty on the numbers}

Coverage (fraction of voxels whose truth falls in the nominal-$L$ interval), expected
calibration error (ECE, mean $|\text{empirical}-\text{nominal}|$ over a level grid) and
sharpness (mean interval width) were re-derived from published definitions in numpy.
\Dstar{} coverage is additionally stratified by the true-\Dstar{} tercile
(edges $0.0511,\,0.0689\times10^{-3}$ mm$^2$/s) to expose conditional failure.
Every load-bearing number carries a bootstrap confidence interval (n\,=\,2000 resamples,
seed 20260621 for the reframe; n\,=\,5000 for Sextant); directional gaps are reported as
significant only when the bootstrap CI excludes zero.

\subsection*{2.6\quad Reproduction verdict}

Gnomon's reproduction driver re-runs the rebuild end-to-end, compares each result to a
frozen target, and emits a verdict. The verdict is PARTIAL: the diagnostic (railing) and
the mechanism reproduce, while two previously reported \emph{marginal} coverage numbers
do not---for the documented and resolved reason of the SD convention above (Section 3.2).
No number in this manuscript is hard-coded; all derive from the live seeded run and are
frozen in \texttt{NUMBERS\_FROZEN.txt}.

% ======================= RESULTS =======================
\section*{3\quad Results}

\subsection*{3.1\quad \Dstar{} boundary-rails in open in-vivo data, and the phenomenon generalises}

On the open OSIPI in-vivo abdomen acquisition, an independently written box-constrained
NLLS fit rails the \Dstar{} estimate at a fit bound in \textbf{54.2\% (95\% CI
52.0--56.4)} of the 1932 homogeneous-ROI voxels (primary $\texttt{rail\_tol}=10^{-3}$),
rising to 56.2\% (54.0--58.4) at the looser $10^{-2}$ tolerance and to 58.7\% on the
b0-SNR$>$25 subset (n\,=\,799). A previously reported rate of 54.7\% on the same data
sits inside this confidence interval---reproduced here without using the original code,
on the full ROI rather than an unstated SNR-cut subset (Figure 1). Railing is therefore a
genuine, code-independent property of the data-plus-optimiser, not an artefact of one
analysis.

The phenomenon generalises across selection, scanner, and organ (Figure 1). On the
\emph{full} abdominal ROI (n\,=\,19{,}652) the rate is 47.8\% (47.1--48.5). Our
primary independent cross-organ test is a multi-subject liver DWI cohort (TCGA-LIHC:
different site, vendor (Siemens 1.5T), and organ, acquired with a proper $b=0$ and no
IVIM ground truth): under a clean four-b-value scheme \Dstar{} rails in 43.7\%
(43.2--44.2) of voxels, reproducing the phenomenon in an entirely unrelated acquisition.
A sparser three-b-value liver scheme rails still more---73.4\% (73.0--73.9)---which we
report as supporting rather than load-bearing evidence: it omits $b=0$, is normalised by
its lowest acquired $b$ (an approximation), and pools only three subjects (per-subject
rates 0.66, 0.74, 0.75, mutually consistent). Its higher rate is directionally consistent
with the mechanism---thinner low-$b$ perfusion sampling weakens \Dstar{} identifiability
further---and the rail is almost entirely to the lower bound, exactly as identifiability
predicts. In the abdomen the rail is predominantly
to the upper bound (39\% upper vs 16\% lower), and a generous-bound control still rails a
third of voxels, confirming railing is not an artefact of tight bounds. All cohorts clear
a pre-registered replication threshold (point rate $\ge 0.30$, CI lower bound
$\ge 0.20$). Weak \Dstar{} identifiability is thus not a simulation abstraction but a
quantitative, reproducible feature of real IVIM acquisitions.

\subsection*{3.2\quad The calibration consequence is conditional, not a uniform marginal collapse}

What does railing do to the uncertainty intervals a clinician would read? A railed,
unidentified \Dstar{} breaks the Gaussian assumption underlying symmetric credible
intervals. Crucially, the failure is \emph{conditional}: it concentrates where \Dstar{}
is large and least constrained (Figure 2, Table 1). Under the honest Cram\'er--Rao
convention, central-95\% \Dstar{} coverage for the symmetric Laplace SD interval falls to
\textbf{0.63 (0.60--0.67)} in the high-\Dstar{} tercile while remaining near-nominal at
low \Dstar{} (0.91); the symmetric MCMC SD interval shows the same shape, 0.81
(0.78--0.84) high versus 0.95 low. Pooled over all terciles these are an undramatic 0.80
and 0.90---which is precisely why a single marginal number misrepresents the failure.

This reframing is deliberate and is the reason the reproduction verdict is PARTIAL. A
previously reported pair of \emph{marginal} \Dstar{} coverage figures (0.30 Laplace,
0.67 MCMC-SD) did not reproduce in the clean rebuild, whose honest marginal values are
0.80 and 0.90. The earlier severity is recovered only under the floored SD convention
(Section 2.4): flooring a railed voxel's SD drops the honest pooled Laplace coverage from
0.80 to 0.68 and the high-\Dstar{} tercile from 0.63 to 0.41 (Figure 2, hatched bars).
The floored convention manufactures the original headline; the honest convention does the
statistically correct thing and widens where information is absent. We therefore retire
the marginal 0.30/0.67 figures as headline numbers and report the conditional table
instead. The finding stands---symmetric Gaussian intervals genuinely under-cover
\Dstar{}---but as a regime-specific, convention-explicit statement.

\subsection*{3.3\quad The resolution: interval shape and amortization}

The fix follows the diagnosis. Because the failure is one of interval \emph{shape}---a
symmetric Gaussian cannot represent a bounded, skewed, railed posterior---reading the
posterior 2.5/97.5 \emph{quantile} interval rather than a symmetric SD interval restores
near-nominal marginal \Dstar{} coverage, \textbf{0.90 (0.89--0.91)}, with $D$ and $f$
already near-nominal (0.95 and 0.96) (Figure 3A). Under the honest convention the
marginal improvement over the symmetric interval is modest---the dramatic gaps reported
previously were a property of the floored convention---but the qualitative lesson holds:
the right interval \emph{shape}, not a wider SD, is the lever. The residual is itself
informative. Even the quantile interval still under-covers the high-\Dstar{} tercile at
\textbf{0.81 (0.78--0.84)} (Figure 2): this surviving gap is the identifiability wall
itself, not an interval-shape artefact, and marks the floor of what any reweighting of a
fixed posterior can achieve.

Amortization improves on the railed baseline on every axis (Figure 3B). On a synthetic
held-out set a clean-room amortized neural posterior estimator is simultaneously
better-calibrated than the railed NLLS baseline (\Dstar{} coverage \textbf{0.98 vs
0.76}; gap 0.216, CI 0.197--0.235), lower-error (ECE \textbf{0.069 vs 0.121}; gap 0.052,
CI 0.036--0.075) and \emph{sharper} (mean width \textbf{0.112 vs 0.181}; gap 0.068, CI
0.060--0.077). All three gaps' bootstrap CIs exclude zero. The railed Gaussian's
wide-but-miscentred intervals lose on calibration and on sharpness at once; the amortized
posterior pays neither price. Together, Sections 3.2--3.3 show the railing pathology is
not merely diagnosable but resolvable: shape-aware intervals recover marginal
calibration, amortization recovers calibration and sharpness jointly, and the irreducible
residual is confined to, and identifies, the high-\Dstar{} regime.

\section*{4\quad Discussion}

\textbf{What is new.} Weak \Dstar{} identifiability has been understood in principle for
years, and that NLLS fits of \Dstar{} are unstable is textbook (Koh et al., 2011;
Gurney-Champion et al., 2018). The contribution here is not that observation. It is the
first systematic, real-data quantification of \Dstar{} \emph{boundary-railing}---an
optimiser-level, assumption-free fingerprint of that weak identifiability---measured on
open in-vivo data, reproduced in an independent clean-room implementation, shown to
generalise across selection, scanner, organ and acquisition scheme with bootstrap CIs,
and paired with an honest, conditional characterisation of the calibration failure it
induces and the shape-aware resolution of that failure. Relative to learned-UQ frameworks
such as Casali et al.\ (2026), which characterise a particular supervised network's
aleatoric/epistemic uncertainty, the object here is estimator-agnostic and anchored in a
reproducible real-data phenomenon; relative to generalized degeneracy-surfacing
frameworks ($\mu$GUIDE; Jallais \& Palombo, 2024) and to simulation-based inference that
reports aggregate calibration in diffusion MRI (Manzano-Patr\'on et al., 2025), our
emphasis is the conditional, convention-explicit honesty of the coverage claim itself.

\textbf{Why railing is the right primitive.} Railing requires no ground truth and no
trust in a noise model---it is read directly from the optimiser's output---which is what
makes it both immune to the over-extension critique that attaches to assumption-laden
calibration claims and directly actionable: a visibly railed voxel is a flagged voxel.
This is also a clinically safer failure mode than the silent alternative. A bounded
least-squares fit that rails announces its own unreliability; an estimator that quietly
reverts to a prior mean under the same weak identifiability produces an artificially
smooth, confident-looking \Dstar{} map that masks the same underlying uncertainty.
Surfacing railing is therefore a feature, not only a defect, of the classical fit.

\textbf{Conditional honesty.} The central methodological lesson is that a marginal
coverage number is the wrong unit for a failure driven by identifiability. Averaged over
a parameter range it understates a regime-specific collapse, and it is sensitive to an
undocumented SD convention that can manufacture an arbitrary severity. Reporting coverage
per true-\Dstar{} tercile, under an explicitly named Cram\'er--Rao convention, makes the
claim regime- and convention-robust: symmetric Gaussian intervals under-cover \Dstar{}
specifically where it is large and least identified, and the residual high-\Dstar{} wall
(0.81) that survives the quantile fix is the identifiability limit, not a fixable
interval-shape artefact.

\textbf{Scope and what is deliberately out of it.} Three lines of inquiry that an earlier,
broader version of this work pursued are deliberately excluded here so that every reported
number traces to the reproduced railing-and-calibration core. (i) A full information-floor
(Cram\'er--Rao) audit of the amortized neural posterior's claimed-versus-achieved
precision---the ``overconfident below the floor'' analysis---is a separate study; here the
amortized posterior appears only as a better-calibrated comparator to the railed baseline
(Section 3.3). (ii) A deployment-time out-of-distribution self-consistency gate and (iii)
an in-vivo brain held-out-b coverage analysis are likewise out of scope; they were not
part of the clean-room reproduction and are not claimed. Restricting scope in this way is
what lets the primary phenomenon stand on open, independently checkable evidence.

\textbf{Limitations.} The conditional-coverage characterisation uses a synthetic substrate
because conditioning on true \Dstar{} requires ground truth, which in-vivo data lack; the
real-data evidence is the railing rate, which needs no ground truth. The honest
Cram\'er--Rao floor is a local Gaussian approximation, least tight exactly where the
\Dstar{} posterior is most skewed; its bias is favourable to our conclusion (the true
achievable variance generally exceeds the Gaussian floor where the posterior is skewed).
The cross-cohort liver replication has no IVIM ground truth and is reported as a railing
generalization, not a parameter-recovery test. A reference-standard cohort (phantom or
repeated-acquisition) remains the natural next step.

\textbf{Conclusion.} \Dstar{} weak identifiability is not only a simulation fact: it
leaves a measurable, reproducible boundary-railing signature in open in-vivo IVIM data
that recurs across cohorts, induces a conditional---not uniform---under-coverage in
symmetric Gaussian intervals, and is resolved by shape-aware (quantile) intervals and
amortized posteriors, up to an irreducible high-\Dstar{} wall. Reporting the phenomenon
conditionally and convention-explicitly is what makes the claim honest and reproducible.

source of truth is the Gnomon clean-room reproduction, regenerated by

in-tree.
