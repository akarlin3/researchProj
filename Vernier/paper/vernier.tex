\begin{abstract}
Diffusion-MRI acquisition design optimises either the \emph{variance} of the point
estimate (the Cram\'er--Rao bound, CRLB) or the \emph{expected information} about the
parameters (Bayesian experimental design, BED / EIG). Neither asks whether the
resulting \emph{uncertainty} is \emph{trustworthy}: whether, after a distribution-free
conformal correction, the reported error bars are well \emph{calibrated}. We pose and
test that question for intravoxel incoherent motion (IVIM) imaging, where the
pseudo-diffusion coefficient $\Dstar$ is notoriously ill-determined. The question is
nontrivial because split-conformal restores \emph{marginal} coverage to nominal for
every acquisition by construction; what it does \emph{not} equalise---conditional
coverage and interval sharpness---is the test. On matched-scan-time, matched-CRLB$(\Dstar)$
b-value schemes that differ only in how they spend a fixed budget, the post-conformal
$\Dstar$ interval width spans a factor of $\numWidthMax/\numWidthMin$
(width-spread $\Delta_{\mathrm{sharp}}=\numDeltaSharp$, $95\%$ CI
$[\numDeltaSharpLo,\numDeltaSharpHi]$) and the high-$\Dstar$ conditional coverage
spans $\numHiCovMin$--$\numHiCovMax$ ($\Delta_{\mathrm{cond}}=\numDeltaCond$, CI
$[\numDeltaCondLo,\numDeltaCondHi]$), even though marginal coverage is held at
$\approx\numPostCovHi$ for all of them. Both gaps clear pre-registered thresholds with
bootstrap CIs excluding zero: \textbf{acquisition design moves the part of calibration
conformal does not fix, at matched precision.} The effect is \emph{estimator-contingent}:
it is large for the over-confident segmented estimator that conformal correction targets
but \emph{collapses} below threshold for an efficient amortised (MAF) posterior, which is
already near-calibrated ($\Delta_{\mathrm{sharp}}\!:\numDeltaSharp\!\to\!\numMafDeltaSharp$,
$\Delta_{\mathrm{cond}}\!:\numDeltaCond\!\to\!\numMafDeltaCond$); the divergence is a
property of the estimator$\times$acquisition interaction, and matters precisely for the
imperfect estimators that dominate clinical IVIM. We are deliberately narrow on a second
axis too: Vernier does \emph{not} claim to improve identifiability---a companion
analysis\PROV{} shows the high-$\Dstar$ wall is acquisition-robust (CRLB-optimal design
moved CRLB$(\Dstar)$/tercile-width only $\numGaugeWallHi\!\to\!\numGaugeWallLo$). The claim
lives on the calibration-and-decision axis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The choice of diffusion weightings (b-values) in an IVIM acquisition is an
experiment-design problem. Two objectives dominate the literature. The first is
\emph{variance-optimal} design: place b-values to minimise the CRLB of the fitted
parameters \cite{lemke2011optimal,zhang2013crlb,jalnefjord2019optimization}. The
second is \emph{information-gain} design: maximise the expected information gain (EIG)
about the parameters under a Bayesian experimental-design (BED) framework
\cite{alexander2008framework,dai2026impulsed,iollo2025activemri}. Both optimise a
property of the \emph{point estimate}---its variance, or the information the data carry
about it.

Neither asks the question a clinician actually relies on: when the method reports an
error bar, can you believe it, and how much is that error bar \emph{worth} per minute
of scan time? Trustworthiness of an interval is \emph{calibration}: does a nominal
$90\%$ interval cover the truth $90\%$ of the time, marginally and within
clinically relevant strata? Distribution-free conformal prediction
\cite{vovk2005algorithmic,lei2018distributionfree,romano2019cqr} restores marginal
coverage for essentially any base estimator. This raises a sharp question that, to our
knowledge, the acquisition-design literature has not posed:

\begin{quote}
\emph{At matched scan-time and matched CRLB precision, do different b-value schemes
yield differently-\textbf{calibrated} uncertainty \textbf{after} conformal
correction---and therefore different decision value per scan-minute?}
\end{quote}

This is not answered by either canon. Marginal coverage is equalised by conformal
correction by construction, so it cannot distinguish schemes; and the CRLB / EIG
objectives never look at calibration at all. We test the question directly. Because the
answer was genuinely unknown to us in advance---and because a negative answer would
mean Vernier is not a standalone contribution but a section of the decision-calibration
companion---we treated it as a pre-registered \emph{feasibility gate}
(\S\ref{sec:gate}), with thresholds fixed before running and no tuning toward a
positive outcome.

\section{Honest scope: calibration, not identifiability}
\label{sec:scope}

We must separate two claims that are easy to conflate. \emph{Identifiability} asks
whether the data determine the parameter at all; for IVIM's $\Dstar$ the answer is
largely no, and---crucially---this does not improve with acquisition design. A
companion conformal-coverage analysis\PROV{} swept clinical, CRLB-optimal, and dense
b-schemes and found the high-$\Dstar$ identifiability wall \emph{acquisition-robust}:
high-$\Dstar$ coverage barely moved ($\numGaugeCovLo\!\to\!\numGaugeCovHi$) and the
ratio CRLB$(\Dstar)$/tercile-width stayed $\ge\numGaugeWallLo$ for every scheme, so
CRLB-optimal design lowered the bound ($\numGaugeWallHi\!\to\!\numGaugeWallLo$) without
removing the wall. We take that wall as given.

Vernier therefore makes \emph{no} identifiability claim. It asks the orthogonal
question of whether acquisition design moves \emph{calibration}---the trustworthiness
and sharpness of the error bar after correction---and the downstream decision value.
Our own CRLB implementation (\S\ref{sec:methods}) reproduces the companion's
$\approx\numGaugeWallLo$--$\numGaugeWallHi$ ratio as a cross-check, but the feasibility
result below does not depend on it.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\paragraph{Cohort and estimator (synthetic, open).}
We generate synthetic IVIM cohorts with the open Caliper toolkit: true parameters
$(D,f,\Dstar)$ drawn from physiologically plausible priors ($\Dstar$ log-uniform on
$[10,100]\times10^{-3}\,\mathrm{mm}^2/\mathrm{s}$, the poorly identified axis), clean
bi-exponential signals on a chosen b-schedule, and Rician noise at a chosen SNR. The
device under test is Caliper's torch-free \emph{segmented} least-squares estimator,
which reports deliberately over-confident, homoscedastic quantiles---the standard
model-based-UQ failure mode conformal is meant to repair. No clinical data are used.

\paragraph{Matched scan-time and matched precision.}
Scan-time is proportional to the number of acquired volumes; we hold it fixed by using
$11$ b-values for every scheme in the gate. Among such schemes we select a subset whose
expected CRLB$(\Dstar)$---computed from the IVIM Fisher information matrix---agree to
within $\pm\numCrlbTolPct\%$ of the candidate-pool median, so neither budget nor
variance can explain a difference between them. Matching precision is conservative: it
removes precision as a confounder and makes any surviving calibration divergence
\emph{harder} to find, not easier.

\paragraph{Conformal correction and the ruler.}
For each scheme we split the cohort into calibration and test halves, fit Caliper's
split-conformal / conformalised-quantile-regression (CQR) correction
\cite{romano2019cqr} on the calibration half, and apply it to the test half. We score
the corrected intervals with a model-agnostic calibration ruler: marginal coverage,
quantile expected calibration error (ECE), sharpness (mean interval width), and
conditional coverage within true-$\Dstar$ terciles \cite{gneiting2007scoring,kuleshov2018calibrated}.

\paragraph{Pre-registered gate.}
The decision was fixed before running. Marginal coverage is \emph{not} the metric: CQR
restores it to nominal for every scheme by construction (we report it only as a sanity
check). The load-bearing quantities are what conformal does not equalise:
\emph{sharpness}, summarised by the across-scheme width spread
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sharp}}=(\max-\min)/\mathrm{median}$ of the post-conformal $\Dstar$
interval width; and \emph{conditional coverage}, summarised by the range
$\Delta_{\mathrm{cond}}$ of high-$\Dstar$-tercile coverage. We declare the question
\textbf{feasible} (PASS) iff $\Delta_{\mathrm{sharp}}\ge\numThreshSharp$ or
$\Delta_{\mathrm{cond}}\ge\numThreshCond$, \emph{and} that gap's $95\%$ bootstrap CI
excludes zero. The bootstrap is \emph{paired}: schemes share the same true parameters
and noise (only the b-scheme differs), and each resample draws both the calibration
indices (which set each scheme's conformal offset, and hence its width) and the test
indices (which set coverage).

The effect is robust to SNR. At SNR~$25$, $\Delta_{\mathrm{sharp}}=\numDeltaSharpSnrLow$
and $\Delta_{\mathrm{cond}}=\numDeltaCondSnrLow$; at SNR~$50$,
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sharp}}=\numDeltaSharpSnrHigh$ (the width gap shrinks as the estimator's
$\Dstar$ error converges) while $\Delta_{\mathrm{cond}}=\numDeltaCondSnrHigh$ persists.
The conditional-coverage divergence is thus the robust load-bearing finding: marginal
conformal correction hides scheme-dependent conditional miscoverage that the
acquisition determines.

\subsection{Efficiency frontier: decision value saturates with scan-time}
\label{sec:frontier}

We next score, through the decision lens of the companion decision-calibration
work\PROV{} \cite{minos2026}, each scheme's conformal-corrected $\Dstar$ posterior on a
stylised treat/spare/escalate decision with asymmetric cost. Sweeping scan-time from
$\numFrontNbSparse$ to $\numFrontNbDense$ b-values, the corrected $\Dstar$ interval
sharpens ($\numFrontWidthSparse\!\to\!\numFrontWidthDense$) and the achieved decision
utility improves, but with steeply \emph{diminishing returns}: the marginal decision
utility per added scan-minute falls $\numFrontMargA\!\to\!\numFrontMargB\!\to\!\numFrontMargC$
across the steps. Crucially, \emph{no} protocol beats the no-scan prior baseline for
$\Dstar$ (every mean utility lies below the prior's $\numPriorBaselineU$)\PROV{}:
acquisition does not rescue $\Dstar$ \emph{decisions}, consistent with the
identifiability wall (\S\ref{sec:scope}). The protocol implication is that the
clinical 11-b acquisition captures most of the recoverable calibrated decision value;
beyond it, additional scan-minutes buy very little, and none of it closes the
$\Dstar$ decision gap.

\subsection{Robustness: the divergence is estimator-contingent}
\label{sec:maf}

The gate above used Caliper's deliberately over-confident segmented estimator. To test
whether the divergence is a property of the \emph{acquisition} or of the \emph{estimator},
we re-ran the identical gate---same matched schemes, train/cal/test splits, metrics, and
paired bootstrap---on Caliper's efficient masked-autoregressive-flow (MAF) posterior, and
on the reference estimator under those same splits. (Both estimators ship with the open
Caliper toolkit, so this experiment is also SOLID.) Under matched splits the reference
still PASSES ($\Delta_{\mathrm{sharp}}=\numRefSplitDeltaSharp$,
$\Delta_{\mathrm{cond}}=\numRefSplitDeltaCond$). The MAF, by contrast, is already
near-calibrated \emph{before} any correction (raw $\Dstar$ coverage $\approx\numMafRawCov$);
its corrected $\Dstar$ intervals are an order of magnitude sharper
($\approx\numMafWidthLo$--$\numMafWidthHi$ vs $\numWidthMin$--$\numWidthMax$); and,
crucially, they barely differ across schemes: $\Delta_{\mathrm{sharp}}=\numMafDeltaSharp$
(CI $[\numMafDeltaSharpLo,\numMafDeltaSharpHi]$) and $\Delta_{\mathrm{cond}}=\numMafDeltaCond$
(CI $[\numMafDeltaCondLo,\numMafDeltaCondHi]$), both below the pre-registered thresholds:
\textbf{\numMafVerdict}. The calibration divergence is therefore not a property of the
acquisition alone but of the estimator$\times$acquisition interaction: it appears when the
estimator's realised error is large and varies with sampling geometry---the over-confident
regime conformal correction exists to repair---and vanishes for an efficient posterior whose
error already tracks the CRLB.

\section{Discussion}

The feasibility result establishes a third axis for diffusion-MRI acquisition design,
distinct from variance (CRLB) and information (BED/EIG): \emph{post-correction
calibration}---but, sharpened by \S\ref{sec:maf}, only for the over-confident estimators
that conformal correction targets. At matched precision and matched scan-time, where the
variance- and information-optimal objectives are by definition indifferent, schemes built
on such an estimator still differ substantially in the sharpness and conditional coverage
of their conformally corrected $\Dstar$ intervals. Because marginal conformal correction
equalises only marginal coverage, these differences are invisible to a marginal-coverage
audit and to the CRLB, yet they are exactly what a downstream decision sees.

We are deliberately careful about what this does and does not show. \textbf{(1)} It is
a calibration claim, not an identifiability claim: the companion wall result\PROV{}
stands, and our own frontier confirms that acquisition does not make $\Dstar$ decisions
beat the prior. \textbf{(2)} The divergence is \emph{estimator-contingent}, not universal
(\S\ref{sec:maf}): it is large for the over-confident segmented estimator but collapses
below threshold for an efficient MAF posterior. We read this as localising the
contribution rather than refuting it---acquisition-aware calibration matters exactly for
the imperfect, over-confident estimators that dominate clinical IVIM and that motivate
conformal correction in the first place, and is unnecessary when a well-calibrated
amortised posterior is already available. \textbf{(3)} The sharpness gap is
SNR-dependent (it shrinks at high SNR), whereas the conditional-coverage gap persists,
so the latter is the more durable signal. \textbf{(4)} ``CI excludes zero'' is a weak
bar for a non-negative range statistic; the substantive criterion is the pre-registered
\emph{magnitude} threshold, which both gaps clear.
